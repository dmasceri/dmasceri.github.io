MORPHFLIGHT

Playable prototype

On-rails cave flyer with a morph-weapon core mechanic

You auto-fly forward through cavernous environments, dodging and aiming with the stick while reshaping your weapon's spread — orb, horizontal disk, vertical disk — to match the enemy formation in front of you. Match the shape and the formation melts and bonuses stack; mismatch it and you only chip away at enemies.

There's a handful of environments to play with that I had fun making after exploring the morph-and-shoot mechanic. By the end of the prototype phase I started to investigate syncing the procedurally-generated environment to music. More to come on that in the next update.

Side note: play with an Xbox controller linked to your PC over a cable or Bluetooth, it's way more fun.

Why I made it: initially, to explore the initial idea — that a two-layer "aim and morph" gameplay loop could carry a shooter. I'd be remiss if I didn't mention my wife reminded me while writing this blog post that Dead Space used exactly this vertical/horizontal weapon morph in an FPS package. Environments and enemies are the real test, and I'll need to hammer on those to see if this can be really fun. This repo is the runnable vertical slice from that prototyping phase. Did I prove out the idea?

"All I know is my gut says maybe." — Futurama

RAMPS

Research archive

Rapid Automated Modeling for the Performance of Structures

MATLAB software that builds and load-rates finite-element models of highway bridges by driving the Strand7 solver through its API — generating the 3D model, running simulated dead- and live-load tests, and computing rating factors with no manual modeling.

A second module that streamlines and automates the model-tuning step by comparing the natural frequencies and mode shapes of the model against a forced-vibration experiment. The software controls Strand7 to run a modal analysis, then uses another non-linear optimization solver in MATLAB to update model parameters — bearing stiffness, deck-to-girder composite action — so the model behaves more like the real structure.

Finally, there's an automated girder-sizing module using AASHTO LRFD and ASD standards. I don't use the term "design" because there's no human intuition, logistical constraints, or other factors that go into what engineers actually build. I coded a 2D FE solver that calculates demands, and a non-linear optimization algorithm finds the smallest girder by material volume that still meets the design specs. This has been verified against both hand calcs and real-life bridges.

This was built during my doctoral research at Drexel and matured at Rutgers. The model-building and updating engine was a companion to the THMPR rapid mobile testing system that has a European patent and won the 2017 ASCE Pankow Award for Innovation.

Why I made it: automate the model-building loop, automate the model-tuning loop, automate the design-and-load-rating loop. I was able to say some impactful things about bridge design and the difference between reality and simulation (dissertation). In the end, it comes down to "all models are wrong, some are useful." It's archived here as written.

Intraday Regime Dashboard

Working tool · v2 in progress

Read-only market regime & intraday observation dashboard

A calibration and observation tool for intraday equity index metrics (SPY / QQQ / IWM) against an Alpaca paper account and free FRED data with no orders or positions. An engine process owns all the data I/O and metric math and writes a permanent calibration log to SQLite; a thin Streamlit UI just reads and renders and always flags when the last data pull happened.

In development now: the action layer, i.e. a per-ticker entry trigger, an on-deck trade setup, and armed/triggered states that show how it'll look when a tradable position is ready or executed. The shipped v1 is the read-only observation grid; the action layer is what I'm building next.

Why I made it: I wanted to watch how regime signals behave through a live session and build intuition before risking real dollars. Also to be able to learn about Docker and ship a two-stage Docker stack. Next step? Training a trading algorithm.
